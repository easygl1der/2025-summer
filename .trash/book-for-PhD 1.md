## Take some downtime; chill; go on holiday

You don't need to work all the time to be successful. 
 
In fact, many people lose their creative streak by working too much.

Your brain needs the downtime to function at full capacity. 

There is a strong neuroscientific evidence that your brain has a limited supply of certain neurotransmitters that are necessary for both systematic problem-solving and creative thinking. 

The most important lessons can come at the most unexpected of times—you don’t get those if you spend twelve hours a day at your computer.

## Coping with the bad times

1. **Don’t deny the feeling.** You may be feeling very down, but also be thinking, ‘Oh, I’m not really depressed—that’s a label used for people with serious problems.’ No. Don’t deny how you feel. You have every right to feel that way. Once you accept it, you can start to do something about it.
2. **Remember your happy times.** Try to recall situations in the past when you were happy, or at least less depressed, and try to replicate those situations. Perhaps they are places you have been, or people you were with, or activities you participated in. Whatever they were, they had an effect on you in the past, so are likely to have a similar effect now.
3. **Surround yourself with positive people.** Some people are emotional ‘vampires’—they suck the happiness out of a room. They might be very cynical, or pessimistic about the world. They might enjoy talking about negative concepts, such as the bad state of international politics. Perhaps they have a habit of finding faults in other people, or processes, and like to talk to you about it. These people are not healthy to be around when you are feeling down. Try to form some friendships or acquaintances with people who are naturally more positive in their behaviours.
4. **Truly believe there is an end to this feeling.** You did not always feel like this. You have to think beyond the time frame you are in now. There will be a time again when you will not have this feeling. This is not permanent. This is not how things will be forever.

The voice is an internal narrative, sliding from facts (your supervisor said something nice) to opinions (you think they didn't read it properly), to judgements (you judge your own work as flawed), to anxiety (you think of yourself as an imposter). This is described wonderfully in a book called 'Emotional Agility', by Susan David.³

She describes how the voice takes you rapidly from the facts stage, to the opinions stage, to the judgements stage, to the anxiety stage, before you even realise. When you are in the anxiety stage, it's difficult to remember that it all came from your internal voice, not an external fact. Making yourself conscious of the voice can help-recognise when it's the voice, and when it's external objective information, that is guiding your thought processes.
